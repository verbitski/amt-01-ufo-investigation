# High-Speed and Transmedium Extrapolation

> **Conceptual sandbox:** This document explores a conditional extrapolation. It is not an observation, recovered technology, established physics, or evidence of what caused the reported AMT-01 event.

## 1. Why friction removal is insufficient

At 20,000–30,000 km/h, an object in dense air does not merely experience surface friction. It compresses, shocks, heats, dissociates, and may ionize the atmosphere.

\[20{,}000\;\text{km/h}=5{,}556\;\text{m/s}\]

\[30{,}000\;\text{km/h}=8{,}333\;\text{m/s}\]

At sea-level air density, dynamic pressure:

\[q=\frac{1}{2}\rho v^2\]

| Speed | Approximate dynamic pressure in air |
|---:|---:|
| 20,000 km/h | ~19 MPa |
| 30,000 km/h | ~43 MPa |

The problem is normal medium interaction, not just skin friction.

## 2. Conditional high-speed architecture

```text
forward precursor / field lens
→ graded medium-conditioning region
→ standoff exclusion envelope
→ protected inner hull
→ wake-restoration region
```

In this model, the effective aerodynamic boundary might be far larger and smoother than a hypothetical inner hull.

## 3. Sonic boom and shock

A rigid invisible vacuum bubble would still create a shock at its outer boundary. To avoid this, the medium must be altered or redirected over a sufficient distance before the boundary arrives.

Therefore, a model claiming low-signature motion at these assumed speeds would require **distributed upstream action**, not only a near-hull gap.

## 4. Energy scale warning

A body with effective cross-sectional area \(A\) sweeping dense air at speed \(v\) encounters mass flow roughly:

\[\dot m=\rho vA\]

A normal mechanical displacement power scale is:

\[P\sim\frac{1}{2}\rho A v^3\]

For several square meters at 8.3 km/s near sea level, this reaches order-terawatt scale. Under these assumptions, a viable low-signature proposal could not simply push the full local air mass aside in an ordinary way.

## 5. Underwater mode

Seawater density is approximately 850 times air density near sea level. At equal speed, dynamic pressure scales with density.

| Speed | Approximate dynamic pressure in seawater |
|---:|---:|
| 20,000 km/h | ~15.8 GPa |
| 30,000 km/h | ~35.6 GPa |

Normal underwater motion at these speeds would generate catastrophic shock, cavitation, heating, and acoustic disturbance.

## 6. Transmedium transition

A hypothetical low-disturbance air-to-water transition would require:

1. boundary formation ahead of the water surface;
2. local exclusion or controlled phase/coupling change;
3. hull passage without direct impact;
4. water-mode pressure boundary activation;
5. controlled closure behind the craft.

A simple gas bubble or ordinary supercavity would still generate bubbles, wake, noise, and medium-specific constraints.

## 7. Altitude dependence

Extreme apparent speeds are much easier to accommodate at very high altitude because atmospheric density is low. Any public high-speed case must therefore establish distance and altitude before it can constrain MEVE/Q-MEVE.

## 8. Relationship to AMT-01

The AMT-01 account reports only slow motion. This high-speed/transmedium model is an extrapolation prompted by separate external claims, not an observation from AMT-01 or evidence that those claims describe the same kind of event.
